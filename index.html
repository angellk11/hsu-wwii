Fallen Reddies of World War II

Reddies served in every corner of the globe during World War II, from Europe and North Africa to India, Alaska, the South Pacific, the Caribbean, and the Atlantic. These are the names of those known to have died in the war—and of one who lived to fight another day. Among these men are the first professional baseball player and the first chaplain from Arkansas to die in WWII. During the conflict, Henderson president Matt Locke Ellis personally wrote letters to console those parents whose sons were killed and to encourage those whose sons were reported missing or injured. Some of the information below comes from those letters and from a draft of a speech he likely gave at the dedication of memorial holly trees along Huddleston Street (since removed). If you know of a Reddie—student, faculty, or staff member—who died in WWII and is not listed here, please contact the library. Corrections or additional details would also be especially appreciated.

Lloyd Buster Bardwell Jr.

Attended:1938-1940 Born:2/12/1920 Hometown:Arkadelphia, AR Branch:Army Air Force Unit:500th Bomber Squadron, 345th Bomber Group (Medium) Rank:Second Lieutenant Serial Number:O-761500 Theater:Pacific Died:9/2/1944 Died How:
KIA Hit by anti-aircraft fire had to ditch plane in the ocean
Died Where:
Lembeh Strait, Indonesia
Buried:
Cenotaph at Rose Hill, Arkadelphia, and on Wall of the Missing in Manila, Philippines
Bio:

2Lt. Bardwell served as a pilot on the B-25D "Mexican Spitfire". He took off from Biak Island, Biak Numfor Regency, Papua Province, Indonesia on a mission over Langoan Airfield, Sulawesi, Indonesia. They were later diverted to strafe Japanese soldiers near Lahbeh Strait, Indonesia. During the mission they were hit by ground anti-aircraft fire which damaged their B-25 enough that they had to ditch into the Lahbeh Strait. On impact, the nose submerged, forcing the tail and right wing to break off. A Japanese Zero strafed the crew as they were escaping, but was driven away by other B-25s from the squadron circling to protect them. All five crew were observed in the water before their plane sank five minutes later. Their raft seemed damaged and only partially inflated. Short on fuel, the other B-25s departed and no trace of the crew was ever seen again. Lloyd was officially declared Missing in Action during the war. He was awarded the Air Medal and the Purple Heart.

Henry Bishop Citty

Attended:1938 Born:4/28/1919 Hometown:Ashdown, AR Branch:Army Unit:Company F, 2nd Battalion, 325th Glider Infantry Regiment, 82nd Airborne Division Rank:Second Lieutenant Serial Number:O1306814 Theater:Europe Died:9/24/1943 Died How:

Died Where:
In Italy, probably near Salerno
Buried:
St. Paul Methodist Church Cemetery; Ozan, AR
Bio:

Lt. Citty entered Henderson in 1938. By 1940, he had completed two years of college and was working as a driver while living at home. He enlisted in the army on December 21, 1940 when his Arkansas National Guard unit was federalized. It became the 153rd Infantry Regiment which was stationed in Alaska. While there he reached the rank of Staff Sergeant. Citty was picked to attend Officers Candidate School and became a Lieutenant in January 1943. That same month he married Christine Phillips. He became a member of the 325th Glider Infantry Regiment. The 325th first saw action when they were sent to Salerno in September 15, 1943, arriving in landing crafts, not gliders, to reinforce Rangers who had landed a week earlier. Lt. Citty was killed on September 24, 1943 as the Allies advanced toward the Volturno River. He was only officer from his regiment killed in action in Italy.

Joseph L."Joe" Cook Jr.

Attended:1939 Born:11/14/1917 Hometown:Stamps, AR Branch:Army Unit:Company K, 3rd Battalion, 115th Infantry Regiment, 29th Infantry Division Rank:Staff Sergeant Serial Number:20740393 Theater:Alaska, France Died:9/19/1944 Died How:

Died Where:
Evacuation Hospital in Brest, France
Buried:
Brittany American Cemetary and Memorial, France
Bio:

SSgt. Cook received his education by attending Stamps Grammar and High school, Hope High School, and Henderson State Teachers College, after which he was employed by his father. He married Lucy Oleva Copeland in Arkansas. Cook joined the Army on December 23rd, 1940 and received his basic training at Camp Robinson, qualifying as a squad leader. After training, he was sent to duty in Alaska for seven months as part of the Alaskan Defense Command. He was then deployed to Europe on May 24th, 1943 and participated in the Normandy invasion. He was slightly wounded June 18th, 1944 at Normandy, more seriously wounded at St. Lô on July 30th, 1944, and was wounded a third time at Brest on September 17th, 1944, which resulted in his death at the Evacuation Hospital there. He received the Purple Heart, Bronze Star, Army Good Conduct Medal, and American Defense Medal.

Miller Gene Halbert

Attended:1938 Born:8/23/19 Hometown:Malvern, AR Branch:Marine Corps Unit:Marine Fighter Squadron 451 "Blue Devils" Rank:Lieutenant Serial Number:O-028250 Theater:Pacific Died:3/30/1945 Died How:
KIA Shot down
Died Where:
Over Naha, Okinawa, Japan
Buried:
Cenotaph in Honolulu, HI and Malvern, AR
Bio:

Lt. Halbert was the son of D. Miller Halbert and Cornelia Little. He graduated from Malvern High School and attended Henderson, Hendrix College, and the University of Arkansas. He was studying law at the University of Arkansas when he entered the U.S. Navy in October, 1941. He saw service in Puerto Rico and the Caribbean area and after one year he transferred to the Marine Corps as an aviation cadet. Lt. Halbert received his flight training at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, and continued his training at Memphis, Tennessee, Pensacola and Miami, Florida and Mojave, California. He received his gold pilot's wings and commission as a fighter pilot on 8/13/1943. He was assigned to fly the famous Marine Chance-Vought F4U-1D Corsair fighter. In January 1945, his Marine Fighter Squadron VMF-451 "Blue Devils" was assigned to the USS Bunker Hill. Lt. Halbert's fighter unit was the first to fly over Tokyo; they also participated in the invasion of Iwo Jima. He was reported as being missing in action on 3/30/1945 when he was shot down over Naha, Okinawa, and was officially declared killed in action on 8/1/1945. His body was never recovered. He was awarded the Air Medal with Gold Star.

A.W. "Dub" Harriman

Attended:1943 Born:10/22/1922 Hometown:Hamburg, AR Branch:Army Air Corps Unit:451 Bomber Squadron, 322 Bomber Group (Medium) Rank:Staff Sergeant Serial Number:Unknown Theater:Europe Died:2/9/1945 Died How:
KIA Shot down
Died Where:
Over Stadtkyll, Germany
Buried:
Luxembourg American Cemetery, Luxembourg
Bio:

SSgt. Alberto Willard Harriman was a graduate of Hamburg High School and Henderson College. He entered the Army Air Corps in October, 1942. Harriman trained at Foster Field, Texas; Ground Mechanics School, Goldsburg, North Carolina; and gunner's school at Fort Myers, Florida. He also trained at Barksdale Field in Shreveport, LA, and in Ireland. He left for overseas in May, 1944, and his unit fought in Brest, France while they were stationed in England. He then transferred to France in September, 1944. An engineer and turret gunner on a B-26 Marauder, Harriman served in Iceland, Ireland, Scotland, England and France. He was awarded the Air Medal with five Oak Leaf Clusters and the Purple Heart. He had completed 30 missions as of December 16, 1944. By the time he was listed as missing and then killed in action January 1, 1945, in Germany, he had completed 35 missions. On January 3, 1946, in a ceremony at Selman Field in Monroe, Mrs. Harriman received the Air Medal with a Silver Oak Leaf Cluster on behalf of her son. In May, 1946, Hamburg VFW Post 4509 changed its name to the Spivey-Harriman Post No. 4509 in honor of James Spivey and A. W. Harriman.

Attended:1937-1939 Born:2/17/1918 Hometown:Camden, AR Branch:Royal Canadian Air Force Unit:153 Squadron (Royal Air Force) Rank:Flight Officer Serial Number:Canadian Service #J/10128 Theater:Britain, Europe, North Africa Died:5/28/1943 Died How:

Buried:

Bio:

F/O Wightman Miles Harris was the son of William Miles Harris and Texa Bama Porterfield. He had three sisters and one brother. Harris attended Camden High School and then went on to complete a certificate program at Henderson State Teachers College before enlisting in the Royal Canadian Air Force on January 3rd, 1941. Harris earned the following decorations for his contribution to the Allied cause: The Canadian Memorial Cross; 1939-1945 Star Medal; Africa Star Medal; The Defense Medal; Canadian Volunteer Service Medal with silver bar and Maple Leaf; War Medal 1939-1945.

Gilbert Glover Lawson, Jr.

Attended:1937-1940 Born:9/14/1918 Hometown:Hot Springs, AR Branch:Army Air Corps Unit:352nd Squadron, 301st Bomb Group, 15th Air Force Rank:Lieutenant Serial Number:Unknown Theater:Europe Died:2/25/1944 Died How:
KIA
Died Where:
Over Regensburg, Germany
Buried:
Greenwood Cemetery, Hot Springs, AR
Bio:

Lt. Lawson was born in Texarkana, Arkansas. He graduated from Hot Springs High School and enrolled at the Henderson State Teachers College and received a degree. He entered the Army Air Corps on December 3, 1941, four days before the Japanese attack at Pearl Harbor. He received his basic training at Maxwell Field, Alabama, and attended Gunnery School at Ft. Myers, Florida, and navigator's school at Selman Field, Louisiana. He qualified as expert in air gunnery and received his gunnery wings, navigator's wings aerial observer’s wings. Gilbert was assigned to a B-17 in the 301st Bomb Group, 352nd Squadron, 15th Air Force and departed the United States for Africa on October 6, 1943. He participated in 35 missions during the African and Italian campaigns to include bombing runs on the German oil fields at Polesti, Romania. He also was in raids over Austria and Germany. Due to his expertise, Gilbert was chosen as navigator for Major Jimmie Doolittle on a special flight through Italy and North Africa in December, 1943. Gilbert was killed in action over Regensburg, Germany during a bombing run on February 25, 1944, when his bomber was shot down. His bomb group received a second Presidential Unit Citation for this mission due to the intense and vicious battle the group had with enemy fighters over Germany. His body was buried in Germany then repatriated after the war and returned to his parents for burial in Arkansas.

Cpt. McKnight was the 6th child of Byron McKnight and his wife Viola Suddath in Lincoln County, Missouri. He was one of four brothers serving during World War II. Percy Elzie Ramsey

Attended:1939-1942 Born:9/21/1917 Hometown:Hope, AR Branch:Army Air Corps Reserve Unit:20th Pursuit Squadron, 24th Pursuit Group Rank:Second Lieutenant Serial Number:O-408845 Theater:Pacific Died:10/24/1944 Died How:

Bio:

Stationed at Nichols Field, 2Lt. Ramsey was taken prisoner when the Philippines surrendered to Japan. He was held in Bataan and loaded aboard the Japanese "hell ship" Arisan Maru en route to Taiwan. On October 21st, the slow Arisan Maru had lagged behind its convoy in the China Sea and was torpedoed by the Allied submarine USS Shark. Ben Waller Rogers Jr.

Attended:1941-1943 Born:6/4/1921 Hometown:Hot Springs, AR Branch:Army Unit:442nd Infantry Regiment, 34th Division Rank:Lieutenant Serial Number:O-528267 Theater:Europe Died:11/14/1944 Died How:
KIA
Died Where:
Saint-Dié, France
Buried:
Epinal American Cemetery and Memorial, France
Bio:

On August 26, 1944 in the vicinity of St. Vallier, Lt. Ben Rogers of the 4th platoon led a patrol to a house and captured 6 Germans. Rogers and the 442nd Regiment, composed largely of Nisei soldiers, was later moved to the Vosges Forest region in eastern France. There they faced the Germans who were fighting with superhuman effort with their backs against the German border. After liberating the French town of Bruyères on October 18, 1944, the regiment was then called upon to rescue the "Lost Battalion", as they suffered decimating casualties in the wintry cold rain and snow. Lt. Rogers. was killed in action in the Vosges Mountain-St Die Campaign on November 14, 1944, during the campaign that eventually liberated the Lost Battalion. Franz Steidl, in his book Lost Battalion, notes that on November 14, 1944, a German self-propelled 88 scored a hit on the 3rd Battalion Command Post killing Lt. Ben W. Rogers, Jr. and wounding Dave Kawagoye, Masao Hayakawa and Masao Aizawa. Rogers was remembered by Anti-Tanker Hany Yoshimura as "a cowboy from Little Rock, Arkansas". He was awarded the Bronze Star Arrowhead, Purple Heart and Combat Infantryman Badge. The Bronze Star Arrowhead designates participation in a combat glider landing

Ben Lehman Rorie

Attended:1937-1940 Born:1/13/1917 Hometown:Hot Springs, AR Branch:Army Air Corps Unit:27th Fighter Squadron, 523d Fighter-Bomber Group, 12th Air Force Rank:Captain Serial Number:O388411 Theater:Alaska, Europe Died:5/27/1944 Died How:
KIA Shot down
Died Where:
Over Rome
Buried:
Masonic Cemetery, Pocahontas, AR
Bio:

Cpt. Rorie graduated from Hot Springs High School and attended Henderson State Teacher's College, where he received a commission as a Second Lieutenant from the ROTC Unit. He enlisted into the regular Army on 12/14/1940, at Ft. Huachuca, Arizona, and received basic infantry training at Ft. Leavenworth, Kansas, and several other installations. He attended Officer Candidate School at Ft. Benning, Georgia. After one year of service as an infantry officer, Ben transferred to the Air Corps and received basic flight training at Kelly and Randolph Fields, Texas, and Grider Field, Pine Bluff, Arkansas. He received his pilot's wings on 8/5/1942 at Kelly Field. He was sent to the Aleutian Islands in mid-summer, 1943 and returned the continental United States in January of 1944. On 1/30/1944 he sailed for Italy and flew 65 missions flying P-47 Thunderbolt fighters with the 27th Fighter Squadron, 523d Fighter-Bomber Group, 12th Air Force. He was made commander of his fighter squadron on 4/9/1944. On 5/27/1944 while flying his 65th mission, he was shot down near Rome by enemy gunfire. He was buried at the Sicily-Rome American Cemetery at Nettuno, Italy, 30 miles from Anzio. His body was repatriated after the war and returned to Arkansas for private burial. Rorie was a lifelong member of the Methodist church and his father and grandfather were both Methodist ministers. He earned the following decorations for his service to the United Sates and the Allied cause in World War II: Distinguished Flying Cross, Air Medal with three oak leaf clusters, Purple Heart Medal, Presidential Unit Citation Ribbon, and the Meritorious Unit Citation sleeve wreathe.

William Earl Tatom

Cpt. Tatom entered Henderson on September 10, 1934 and received the B.A. degree on May 20, 1940. He was killed by machine gun fire as he was getting out of his LVT during the battle for Tarawa, November 20. His body was never recovered. Cpt. Tatom was a member of the Methodist Church.
